# Vitamin B12 (Cobalamin)

## Overview

Vitamin B12 (cobalamin) is a water-soluble vitamin that plays a crucial role in brain development, cognitive function, and the maintenance of the nervous system. The paper highlights B12 as essential for methylation processes and brain health, with particular relevance for ADHD and cognitive function.

## Role in Brain Health

Vitamin B12 is essential for:

- **Methylation processes** critical for neurotransmitter synthesis
- **Myelin synthesis** and maintenance of the myelin sheath
- **Conversion of methylmalonyl-CoA to succinyl-CoA**, a key step in mitochondrial energy production
- **DNA synthesis and repair**
- **Red blood cell formation**
- **Nerve function and protection**

## Deficiency and Brain Health

### Consequences of Deficiency

- **Methylmalonic acid buildup** and odd-chain fatty acids, which are neurotoxic
- **Myelin sheath damage** leading to neurological symptoms
- **Memory loss and brain fog**
- **Depression and mood disorders**
- **Early signs of dementia**
- **Psychosis in severe cases**

### Risk Factors

- **Vegetarian and vegan diets** (commonly deficient)
- **Older adults** (reduced absorption capacity)
- **Individuals with gut absorption issues**
- **Those with MTHFR polymorphisms**

## Research Evidence

Studies have shown that B12 supplementation can:

- Lower plasma homocysteine concentrations by **22%** when taken alone
- Work synergistically with fish oil to lower homocysteine by **39%** when combined
- Support methylation pathways critical for cognitive function
- Improve symptoms in individuals with ADHD and cognitive impairments

### Absorption Rate

- **Highly variable** (14â€“18% from supplements)
- Requires intrinsic factor for absorption
- Better absorbed from food sources than supplements

### RDA (Recommended Daily Allowance)

- **2.4 mcg** for adults
- **2.6 mcg** for pregnant women
- **2.8 mcg** for breastfeeding women

### Supplementation Notes

- Many supplements provide more than the RDA to compensate for low absorption rates
- Methylcobalamin form may be more bioavailable for some individuals
- Regular monitoring of B12 status is recommended for at-risk populations

## Food Sources

### Animal Sources (Highest Bioavailability)

- **Liver and organ meats** (highest concentration)
- **Clams and shellfish**
- **Fish** (salmon, tuna, cod)
- **Eggs**
- **Dairy products** (milk, cheese, yogurt)
- **Beef and poultry**

### Plant Sources (Limited)

- **Fortified plant milks**
- **Nutritional yeast** (fortified)
- **Wolffia globosa** (duckweed - contains some B12)
- **Fermented foods** (may contain small amounts)

## Integration with BRAIN Diet

The BRAIN diet addresses B12 needs through:

- **Omnivore version**: Strategic inclusion of nutrient-dense animal foods
- **Vegan version**: Emphasis on fortified foods and potential supplementation
- **Focus on methylation support** through B12 and other B vitamins
- **Recognition of B12 as a key nutrient gap** in plant-based diets

## Synergistic Effects

Vitamin B12 works synergistically with:

- **Folate (B9)** for methylation processes
- **Vitamin B6** for homocysteine metabolism
- **Omega-3 fatty acids** for cognitive benefits
- **Other B vitamins** for comprehensive methylation support

## Clinical Considerations

### Testing

- **Serum B12 levels** (may not reflect tissue status)
- **Methylmalonic acid** (more sensitive indicator)
- **Homocysteine levels** (elevated in B12 deficiency)

### Supplementation Strategy

- **Regular monitoring** of B12 status
- **Adequate dosing** to overcome absorption limitations
- **Consideration of individual factors** (age, diet, health status)

## Notes

Vitamin B12 is a critical nutrient in the BRAIN diet's approach to supporting brain health. Its role in methylation, myelin synthesis, and mitochondrial function makes it essential for addressing the biological dysfunctions commonly seen in ADHD and other neurocognitive conditions. The diet's flexible approach allows for both animal-based and fortified plant-based sources to meet individual needs and preferences.
