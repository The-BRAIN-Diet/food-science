## Overview

Oysters are bivalve shellfish providing complete protein together with high levels of **zinc**, **vitamin B12**, **iron**, and **selenium**, as well as modest amounts of omega-3 fatty acids. Among commonly eaten foods, oysters are notable for their zinc density, supporting enzymes involved in neurotransmitter synthesis, antioxidant defence, and immune function [1]. Their B12 and iron content contribute to red blood cell formation and one-carbon metabolism, which are important for neurological and cardiovascular health.

Within the BRAIN Diet framework, oysters are considered a **concentrated shellfish source** of zinc and B12 that can complement both animal and plant dietary patterns. Because they are often eaten in smaller portions and less frequently, oysters are best thought of as a periodic nutrient-dense addition rather than a staple protein source [1]. As with other shellfish, water quality, harvesting practices, and preparation determine safety and suitability; sourcing from monitored waters and handling correctly reduces microbiological and contaminant risk [2].

## Food Context

### Sourcing

- Choose oysters from reputable producers that harvest from monitored coastal waters with active testing for biotoxins and contaminants.
- Farmed oysters typically have a low environmental footprint and can help maintain water quality by filter-feeding, but they should still be sourced from regions with robust safety monitoring.

### Serving

- Oysters can be eaten cooked or, where safe and culturally appropriate, raw; steaming or baking reduces microbiological risk while retaining much of their micronutrient density.
- Pair oysters with vegetable-rich accompaniments such as lemon, herbs, and leafy greens to integrate their concentrated mineral content into balanced meals.
